# Scene: scene_9201

## Title
The Priestess in the Mist

## Location
Dream

---

## Scene Text

Night water rocks the hull in long, even breaths.

You sleep before the harbor lights are fully gone.

You stand barefoot on wet stone in a pale, windless mist.

An island surrounds you, though it feels less anchored than remembered, as if it has drifted here only for this hour.

Shapes move behind the fog line: ridges, trees, a broken stair, then nothing. The shoreline seems to shift each time you look away.

A woman in layered gray robes waits beside a low standing stone.

Her face is calm, lined with salt and weather, neither young nor old. Around her neck hangs a small pendant: a circle with a single dark bead set at its center.

She watches you the way someone watches a tide marker.

"You have come with the returning water," she says.

You ask what has troubled you lately.

Why certain hours feel already lived.

Why some harbors seem familiar before your boots touch the planks.

Why the same hush returns, and returns again.

The priestess turns her palm upward. Mist gathers there, then thins.

"Some moments do not pass in a straight line," she says. "They fold. They wait in the seam and come back with a different tide."

She looks toward the hidden surf.

"Some lives are the dream of another life, and some dreams are only the outer room of waking."

The island gives a slow, almost imperceptible shiver beneath your feet.

"There are islands that keep to the open chart," she says, "and islands that drift near the seam."

"Those who sleep lightly hear them first."

You ask whether this has happened before.

She almost smiles.

"Before is a harbor word," she says. "Out here, the water remembers in circles."

The stone beside her bears a faint carving under salt stain.

A circle.

And within it, a single dot.

Fog thickens. Her outline grows thin, as if the mist is taking her shape back.

"When the hour repeats," she says softly, "do not fear it. Listen for what changed."

The sea answers with a low bell-tone from somewhere you cannot see.

You wake to gulls, rope-wood creak, and the ordinary smell of harbor tar.

The dream recedes, but not completely.

Somewhere beneath the morning sounds, a rhythm lingers like a tide returning to an old mark.
